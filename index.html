RELI 102 Glossary

Abrahamic

A term used to encapsulate Judaism, Christianity, and Islam (among other, smaller faiths) that possess a shared history and cast of prophets. Though they split off at various times throughout history, all trace their lineage to Abraham and his sons. This term can be controversial, as practices, beliefs, ideologies, and understandings of prophets – even Abraham – differ between them

Denomination

A subgroup within a larger religion that shares certain traditions and activities considered important for their practice of faith. These may also be called “branches” or “sects” depending on the religion and context. Even adherents of religions with multiple denominations may identify as non-denominational, often not following any one set of traditions

Diaspora

A group of people who live outside of their homeland, often though not always involuntarily and typically as a minority, while retaining aspects of their homeland’s culture. Living in diaspora has heavily influenced Judaism both in the past and present, the African diaspora in Haiti led to Vodou, and practices of Shinto were brought to the United States by the Japanese diaspora

Monotheism

The belief in only one God, as opposed to polytheism. This one God is typically considered transcendental and all-powerful. However, this God can take on multiple forms in some religions, such as with the Trinity in Christianity, and may use supernatural messengers or intermediaries, such as angels in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam or the lwa in Vodou

Mysticism

Esoteric or occult beliefs and practices related to the transcension of ordinary human understandings of the divine, such as by direct communication, attaining understanding of the essence of, or union with a deity. Sufi is a mystical approach to Islam, Kabbalah is a mystical form of Judaism, Gnosticism was a mystical branch present in early Christian history, and Vodou involves the mystic practice of mounting

Polytheism

The belief in multiple gods, making up a pantheon, and with different worship practices for different gods. Believers do not necessarily worship all the gods believed to exist, and these gods can be representations of forces of nature, ancestor spirits, political leaders, or some combination thereof – such as with persona and nature kami in Shinto

Prayer

A practice of communicating with or speaking to a deity. Used for many purposes, such as giving thanks, confessing sins, or requesting blessings. Can be practiced individually or in a group, either out loud or silently. Many religions have some practice of prayer, with either set or personal prayers

Prophet

One who has truths of a faith revealed to them and speaks on behalf of a deity. Often sent to bring new revelations, correct improper practice, or guide a new group of believers. Christianity and Islam reinterpret prophets from Judaism, such as Abraham, Noah, and Moses; Islam considers the Prophet Muhammed to be the most important and sees Jesus as a historical prophet, in contrast to Christian doctrine

Proselytizing

The act of attempting to convert a person or people to one’s own religion. Differentially practiced across Christianity and Islam depending on denomination and culture, and commonly associated with Evangelical Protestantism today. Judaism, Shinto, and Vodou, meanwhile, do not actively seek converts

Ritual

An established religious practice, performed in set ways and usually at specific times. Reading aloud from the Torah, Bible, or Qur’an in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, respectively, is a ritual, as are drawing veve and mounting ceremonies in Vodou, and New Year’s temple visits and offerings to kami in Shinto

Sacrifice

An offering of a life or material object made to a deity to pay homage or invoke their power or favor. Often associated with ritual animal sacrifice, but sacrifices can also be oil, incense, food such as grains or herbs, or other objects. Sacrifice is practiced in Vodou and was practiced in pre-Rabbinic Judaism

Sin

A violation of divine law. Understood differently across different religions, sins may invoke punishment or suffering, either in life or after death, and can differ in severity. One may be able to repent or atone for sins, either through ritual or righting wrongs. May cause impurity and require cleansing, such as in Shinto

Spirit

The incorporeal essence of a person, being, or creature that is often thought to persist even after death. Depending on religion, they may be considered to exist on Earth, move on to a spirit world, or both. Belief in spirits is common in religions, such as ancestor spirits in Shinto being considered a type of kami, while spirits in Vodou can exist among the living

Syncretism

The blending and reconciliation of disparate faith and cultural traditions and practices. Can be seen in the association between African deities and Catholic saints in Vodou, with Islam adopting and adapting local forms of art (such as dance and architecture) and worship as it spread, or vernacular forms of worship in Christianity

Vernacular

The language and dialect spoken in a particular region, which is non-standard and considered informal and of lower status than codified, prestige forms of language. Worship performed in vernacular has historically been marginalized, such as with Catholicism using Latin rather than reading scripture in translation
